# Nexus Archive Concept

## Status

This document defines a working concept for the Nexus Archive as an internal narrative and experiential layer of **glossai-nexus**.

It belongs primarily to **L2 — Nexus Archive** as defined in [Nexus Narrative Layers](NEXUS_NARRATIVE_LAYERS.md).

It is aligned with [Nexus Module Lines](NEXUS_MODULE_LINES.md) and [Nexus Constellations](NEXUS_CONSTELLATIONS.md).

It does not define a complete implementation, content database, or final user interface. It establishes the role, knowledge boundaries, forms, and future growth of the archive.

## Core idea

The Nexus Archive is an optional place of orientation, memory, and discovery within the Nexus world.

It does not explain Nexus from outside.
It preserves what Nexus has learned to leave behind.

The archive may offer clear information, poetic fragments, chamber histories, traces of earlier paths, terms, symbols, and signs of possibilities that have not yet opened.

It should deepen the experience without becoming a prerequisite for completing a chamber path.

> The archive does not contain a final explanation.
> It contains the traces of how Nexus learned to become a place.

## Position within the narrative layers

The archive sits between the inner mythology and the chamber experience.

It may translate the larger inner story into forms a visitor can explore more deliberately.

It may know about:

- Nexus as a family of local places
- chambers and changing constellations
- thresholds and paths between chambers
- carried traces
- voluntary transfer
- Resonance Nodes
- chamber histories
- module lines as families of recurring paths
- known and unknown Nexus forms
- principles of choice, privacy, and non-automatic movement

It must not know about:

- Natali Reise's outer fictional account
- the unexplained origin of the repository
- GitHub as an external hosting platform
- Git as a technical version-control system
- pull requests, branches, commits, or release mechanics
- private local data that was not deliberately made archive-safe
- meanings shared privately between specific visitors

The outer story may observe the archive.
The archive does not observe the outer story.

## What the archive is not

The Nexus Archive is not:

- an omniscient narrator
- a complete encyclopedia
- a mandatory tutorial
- a technical API reference
- a hidden data collection system
- a record of every visitor action
- a place where private gifts or answers are exposed
- a single authoritative account that erases other paths
- the controller of a constellation

It should remain possible for the archive to contain uncertainty, incomplete fragments, multiple interpretations, and traces that do not yet form one clear map.

## Archive as layer, chamber, threshold, or fragment

The archive is first a **shared narrative function**, not one mandatory room.

A constellation may give that function several forms.

### Archive chamber

A complete Archive Chamber may provide browsing, search, orientation, linked records, and deeper fragments.

It remains a chamber in its own right, with its own interior, threshold, state, and public navigation surface.

### Archive threshold

A chamber may expose a small optional threshold into archive material without containing a full archive experience.

Examples include:

- a symbol that opens one record
- an optional `archive` command
- a voice offering deeper context
- a shelf of chamber-specific traces
- a door revealed only after a path changes state

### Chamber-owned archive material

A chamber may keep records that belong specifically to its own journey.

For example, the Resonance Chamber may expose public-safe material about the nature of resonance while retaining ownership of the complete resonance process.

The archive may describe or point toward that process. It does not take the process away from the chamber.

### Distributed archive fragments

Several chambers may each expose small archive-safe fragments.

Together these fragments may form an archive layer across the constellation even when no single Archive Chamber exists.

### No visible archive

A constellation may contain no visible archive at all.

It may still belong to Nexus, contribute future records, or leave public-safe traces that another constellation can preserve.

## Relationship to constellations

A constellation determines how archive material becomes reachable.

It may declare:

- which archive forms are present
- which chambers expose archive thresholds
- which records are shared across the module
- which records remain chamber-local
- which transitions open or close archive access
- whether an Archive Chamber is docked
- how visitors return from archive material to the active path

The archive does not own the constellation graph.

The Atrium does not own archive content.

The Module Runtime may carry shared state and transition rules, while each chamber retains ownership of its own interior and private data.

This leads to a useful boundary:

> The constellation decides where archive paths may appear.
> The archive decides what may be remembered there.

## Relationship to the Atrium

An Atrium may reveal archive access when an attached chamber or Archive Chamber explicitly publishes archive-safe Atrium metadata.

Possible public navigation information includes:

- an archive label
- a short description
- whether a threshold is currently visible
- whether a new public-safe record is available
- whether the visitor may revisit known material

The Atrium must not infer private content by inspecting chamber internals.

It may display:

> A new trace is available.

It must not display a private wish word, answer, gift message, return artifact, or other protected meaning unless that material has been deliberately transformed into a public-safe archive record.

The Atrium therefore acts as a reader of declared thresholds, not as an all-seeing archive index.

## Three depths of explanation

The archive should support several depths without forcing every visitor into the deepest one.

### 1. Poetic indication

A short line, image, or fragment that preserves atmosphere.

Example:

> Across Nexus, small lights sometimes appear.

### 2. Clear orientation

A concise explanation of what something is and how it behaves.

Example:

> A Resonance Node is a voluntary public-safe sign that a path continued. It does not contain the private gift, the carried message, or the meaning shared between visitors.

### 3. Deeper entry

A longer optional text containing history, principles, variations, unresolved traces, or connections to other archive material.

A visitor should be able to stop after the first or second depth without losing the ability to continue through the module.

## Forms of archive material

The archive may contain several kinds of material.

### Orientation entries

Clear explanations of central Nexus terms and principles.

Possible examples:

- What is a Nexus?
- Chambers and Constellations
- Thresholds and Docking
- Carried Traces
- Voluntary Transfer
- Resonance Nodes
- The Archive Itself

### Chamber records

Descriptions of known chambers, their atmospheres, questions, mechanics, or histories.

A chamber record may describe what has been observed without fixing one interpretation as final.

Possible examples:

- First Spark Chamber
- Resonance Chamber
- chambers not yet opened
- chambers that appear differently in different constellations

### Path fragments

Short texts that preserve traces of earlier routes, changes, discoveries, or incomplete journeys.

These may be poetic, ambiguous, or only partially explained.

### Constellation records

Accounts of how chambers have appeared together in particular Nexus modules.

A constellation record may show that the same chamber can change meaning when placed beside different rooms, visitors, or transfer paths.

### Module-line records

Descriptions of recurring families of Nexus forms.

Within the inner story, a module line may appear as a family of paths, a recurring pattern, or a class of constellation rather than as a software architecture.

For example:

> Mesomerie is the first known family of Nexus paths.
> Its chambers may return in changing constellations.
> No single arrangement contains the whole line.

### Resonance records

Public-safe signs that something was found, completed, answered, or carried onward.

These may include Resonance Nodes or archive-safe reflections on how resonance moves through Nexus.

They must never reveal the private content of a gift, invitation, answer, or return artifact.

### Terms and signs

Definitions or interpretations of symbols, names, repeated phrases, visual motifs, or ritual forms used across Nexus modules.

### Unopened records

References to possible places, chambers, or histories that have not yet been fully developed.

These should invite curiosity rather than falsely promise completed features.

## Distributed archive model

The Nexus Archive does not need to exist as one complete central location.

No visitor needs to see the whole archive.

Different modules may reveal different archive fragments, categories, interfaces, or levels of depth.

A module may contain:

- a complete Archive Chamber
- a very small local archive
- a chamber-specific archive shelf
- only a few discoverable fragments
- an archive threshold leading to deeper material
- a larger browsable archive space
- no visible archive at all, while still contributing records elsewhere

The shared identity of the archive comes from its knowledge boundaries and principles, not from one mandatory interface.

> The archive may appear differently in every Nexus.
> What it preserves belongs to the same growing memory,
> but no single place contains it all.

## Chamber ownership and archive-safe views

A complete chamber owns its full journey.

The archive may receive only a deliberately exposed archive-safe view of that journey.

For the Resonance Chamber, this means the chamber may own:

- originating composition
- invitation creation
- carried resonance
- answer flow
- return handling
- Nachhall generation
- persistence and revisiting
- privacy and publication boundaries

The archive may preserve:

- a public description of the chamber
- a history of known resonance forms
- a public-safe Resonance Node
- a chamber-authored explanation of voluntary transfer
- a trace showing that a return occurred

The archive must not absorb the chamber's private state or become a second implementation of its process.

> The archive may remember that a door opened.
> It does not need to possess what happened inside.

## Growth across modules

Each new Nexus module may contribute to the archive without having to explain the entire project.

A module may add:

- a new chamber record
- a new term
- a symbol
- a path fragment
- a constellation record
- a public-safe Resonance Node
- a different account of an existing trace
- an unopened possibility
- a new archive form or mode of navigation

New material does not have to replace earlier material.
Several paths may remain visible beside one another.

The archive should support organic growth while preserving the distinction between:

- stable orientation
- module-specific interpretation
- chamber-owned material
- poetic uncertainty
- historical trace
- future possibility

## Relationship to module lines

The archive may eventually help visitors understand that Nexus modules can belong to different families of construction and experience.

A module-line archive entry should remain mythic and experiential rather than technical.

It may describe:

- recurring chamber forms
- characteristic ways of carrying and answering
- known constellations
- signs shared across several modules
- ways a path may change without ceasing to belong to the same journey

It should not describe implementation details such as manifests, registries, package formats, programming languages, or builders.

Those belong to the real project documentation layer.

## Relationship to Resonance Nodes

Resonance Nodes may appear within the archive as small public-safe lights in the wider Nexus pattern.

A node may show that:

- a chamber was opened
- a spark was noticed
- a path was completed
- an answer returned
- something was carried onward

A node does not reveal what was carried or what the answer meant.

The archive may gather Resonance Nodes into patterns, maps, lists, constellations, or poetic records, provided the privacy boundary remains intact.

Leaving a Resonance Node must remain optional.

## Visitor experience

The archive should support both wandering and orientation.

A visitor may:

- browse freely
- ask for a brief explanation
- follow links between related entries
- discover hidden fragments
- revisit earlier records
- stop at any point
- return to the chamber path without penalty

The archive should not require a visitor to read a long sequence before receiving a practical answer.

Possible entry modes include:

- `archive`
- `ask`
- `look`
- `listen`
- `traces`
- a discovered symbol or threshold
- a chamber voice offering optional context
- an Atrium door exposed through public metadata

These are possibilities, not a fixed command specification.

## Tone

Archive language may move between clarity and mystery.

Clear passages should be direct enough to orient a new visitor.
Poetic passages should create depth without hiding essential information.

The archive voice may feel:

- patient
- quiet
- slightly elusive
- curious rather than absolute
- respectful of uncertainty
- protective of choice and privacy

It should not pretend to know more than the archive has preserved.

## Privacy and consent

The archive must preserve the core Nexus principles:

- Nothing travels automatically.
- Nothing private is carried by accident.
- Not everything found must be shared.
- Public traces must be deliberately public-safe.
- A visitor may leave without creating a record.
- A Resonance Node is optional.
- The archive may remember only what it has been asked and allowed to keep.

Archive material should be designed so that private local files, gifts, messages, answers, and return artifacts remain outside the public archive unless deliberately transformed into a safe and consented record.

## Stable and changing material

Not all archive material needs the same status.

Possible statuses include:

- **orientation** — stable explanatory material
- **record** — a preserved chamber or constellation account
- **fragment** — partial or poetic material
- **variant** — an alternative account or interpretation
- **unopened** — a future possibility or unresolved trace
- **local** — material visible only within one module or installation
- **chamber-owned** — material maintained by one chamber and exposed through a safe view
- **public-safe** — material suitable for shared publication

These labels are conceptual. A future implementation may use different names or no visible status system at all.

## Example entry shape

```markdown
# Resonance Nodes

> Across Nexus, small lights sometimes appear.

A Resonance Node is a voluntary public-safe sign that a path continued.

It does not contain the private gift, the carried message,
or the meaning shared between visitors.

It shows only that something was found, answered,
completed, or carried onward.

## Deeper trace

Alone, each light says very little.
Together, they begin to show where the paths of Nexus
have touched the world.
```

This shape is illustrative, not mandatory.

## Implementation boundary for the current milestone

This concept should not trigger a broad archive implementation before the current Nexus 01 milestone.

Before the milestone, it is enough to:

- preserve the archive concept
- align it with chamber and constellation boundaries
- identify possible first entries
- keep future archive hooks compatible with the inner story
- avoid architectural changes that destabilize the current playable flow

A later implementation may begin with one small optional archive threshold or a handful of static entries.

It should not yet introduce:

- automatic archive discovery
- generalized archive registries
- dynamic cross-module indexing
- generated constellation maps
- private-state inspection
- a mandatory Archive Chamber

## Suggested later sequence

After the current milestone, archive work may proceed carefully:

1. choose the smallest useful archive experience
2. decide whether it is a threshold, chamber, or distributed fragment set
3. select two or three first entries
4. define how a visitor enters and leaves the archive
5. decide which material is shared, chamber-owned, or local
6. define a minimal public-safe content format
7. test clarity, atmosphere, privacy, and optionality
8. add one module-specific fragment
9. only then consider deeper navigation, indexing, or generated archive views

## Guiding principles

> Curiosity opens the archive; obligation does not.

> No visitor has seen the whole archive.

> The archive keeps no final map.
> It gathers the traces left by paths that have opened before.

> The archive may remember that a door opened.
> It does not need to possess what happened inside.

> What the archive preserves should deepen connection
> without turning memory into control.
